“Human Function in the Age of its Technological Reproducibility”

Our planet is depleting from being overworked,
yet we consider it a sickness to be slow?

β. Retardation

[excerpt]

[...] we must note that retardation is defined in isolated terms, without an opposite. In human development, while there are clear marks for what it means to slow down, and — aside from in late stages of adolescence, where we allow the adult appraisals of intelligence or athleticism — there is no such thing as speeding up.

No infant learns to speak at four months, to walk at five, and to solve differential equations the week after. Similarly, few infants learn to read before they speak, or summersault before they crawl, and should they do so, it would likely not be considered as an acceleration, but something else entirely.

No, the opposite to retardation would be constituted by an entirely different line of development. A being who learned how to write at an expert level before they spoke, perform advanced calculations before they read, and could assemble complex machinery before they walked — A being of mind, before body.

Only this could in the purest sense cause an inverse to retardation — as it would shift the line of what is slowed. Eventually, encompassing all human function. A shift that would make most humans, save perhaps a select few experts, and of course the poets to comfort us, become irredeemably as unemployable and unworthy of their share, as the people we have called retarded are to us, right now.
